Theo van Stratum, PhD is a researcher, author, and software practitioner with more than three decades of experience in Python and software development. His work sits at the intersection of probabilistic reasoning, statistical governance, and academic research methodology ‚Äî applying the rigour of actuarial science to the messy, human reality of how software projects are planned and delivered.

His empirical research is grounded in data from over 200 real-world software projects, analysed without the comfortable fictions of story-point velocity and dashboard green lights. He is one of the few practitioners who has spent his entire career watching organisations confuse the performance of confidence for the substance of it ‚Äî and who has built rigorous, Python-powered frameworks to replace guesswork with statistical truth.

In academic publishing, Theo is developing two textbooks for researchers: one on Pandas for Academics, bringing Python data analysis to the workflows of empirical researchers across all disciplines, and one on Probabilistic Python, introducing Bayesian reasoning and uncertainty quantification for academic use.

Actuarial Software Governance

Applying the statistical frameworks of actuarial science ‚Äî P85 confidence thresholds, reference class forecasting, tail-risk quantification ‚Äî to the governance of software delivery. The central thesis: software project failure is not a talent problem; it is a measurement problem.

Probabilistic Reasoning in Organisations

How cognitive biases, the Gambler's Fallacy, and the misuse of statistics degrade decision-making at every level of software organisations ‚Äî and how Bayesian updating and deliberate probabilistic design can systematically correct them.

Python for Academic Research

Bridging the gap between the Python scientific stack and academic researchers ‚Äî with a focus on reproducible workflows, pandas-based data analysis, and Bayesian inference using PyMC and ArviZ for empirical researchers across all disciplines.

Technical Debt as Probabilistic Risk

Quantifying and pricing technical debt using the same frameworks applied to delivery risk ‚Äî moving the conversation from metaphor to measurement, and from complaint to fiduciary accountability.
