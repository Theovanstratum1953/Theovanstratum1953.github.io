Audience: Software leaders, project managers, product managers, and anyone who makes decisions under uncertainty.

Every organisation believes it makes rational decisions. The evidence disagrees.

This book is a systematic investigation into the cognitive biases and statistical misconceptions that degrade decision-making in software organisations — not occasionally, as edge cases, but structurally, as the default. The Gambler’s Fallacy is the most famous of these: the belief that past random events influence future probabilities. But it is only the beginning.

The Problem This Book Solves

Software organisations are making consequential decisions — hiring, architecture, delivery timelines, product strategy — using intuitions that are systematically, predictably wrong. The problem is not that people are irrational. The problem is that the human brain was not designed for the kind of probabilistic reasoning that modern software governance requires. We mistake patterns for signals. We anchor on irrelevant numbers. We weight recent evidence too heavily. We confuse the absence of evidence with evidence of absence.

None of this is stupidity. All of it is correctable — with the right tools.

The Biases Covered

This book examines twenty cognitive biases and statistical misconceptions in depth, with case studies drawn from software development, product management, and technology strategy:

The Gambler’s Fallacy — why “we’ve had three bad sprints, we’re due a good one” is not just wrong but structurally dangerous when built into planning assumptions.

The Hot Hand Fallacy — why the team that just shipped brilliantly is not more likely to ship brilliantly next time, and what that means for resource allocation.

Anchoring Bias — why the first estimate in a planning session contaminates all subsequent estimates, and how to structure conversations that resist this.

Survivorship Bias — why the AI success stories, the startup unicorns, and the “10x developer” narratives you’re reading are a systematically distorted sample.

The Normal Distribution Myth — why software project durations are not normally distributed, and why treating them as if they are produces catastrophically wrong forecasts.

Planning Fallacy — why teams consistently underestimate timelines even when they know about this bias, and what reference class forecasting does that self-awareness cannot.

Confirmation Bias in Code Review — why reviewers find fewer bugs in code from developers they respect, and what that means for quality governance.

Base Rate Neglect — why “our team is different” is the most expensive phrase in software development.

The Bayesian Correction

Each bias is paired with a Bayesian correction: a structured tool for updating beliefs systematically rather than intuitively. Bayesian reasoning is not a mathematical abstraction in this book — it is a practical discipline for decision-making under uncertainty.

The goal is not to turn software teams into statisticians. It is to give decision-makers a set of habits and frameworks that systematically counteract the intuitions that lead organisations astray. Recognising the bias is necessary but not sufficient. Having a replacement process is what changes outcomes.
