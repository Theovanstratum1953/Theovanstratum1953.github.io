Audience: Researchers, PhD students, and academics across all empirical disciplines who work with data.

Academic research runs on data. But the tools most researchers learn first — Excel, SPSS, Stata — were never designed for the rigorous, transparent, reproducible workflows that modern science demands.

This book teaches the pandas library from the ground up, using the datasets, questions, and analytical challenges that researchers actually encounter. It is not a data science book repurposed for academics. It is written for researchers, structured around the research lifecycle, and assumes no prior programming experience beyond basic familiarity with the concept of code.

Why Pandas for Researchers

The reproducibility crisis in science is, at its core, a tooling crisis. Research that cannot be independently replicated — because the analysis steps are buried in point-and-click interfaces, undocumented transformations, or proprietary software — is research that cannot be trusted.

Pandas, running in Jupyter notebooks, provides something that Excel and SPSS fundamentally cannot: a complete, readable, shareable record of every analytical decision from raw data to published result. Every filter, every transformation, every aggregation is documented in code that a collaborator, reviewer, or future researcher can run and verify.

This book teaches that workflow from scratch — not as an abstract technical skill, but as a concrete research practice.

What Makes This Book Different

Most pandas resources are written for data scientists or software engineers. The examples involve sales data, user behaviour logs, and machine learning pipelines. The problems they solve are not the problems researchers face.

This book uses research data throughout: survey datasets, experimental results, longitudinal cohort data, bibliometric data, public health records, economic indicators. The analytical questions are research questions: How do you handle missing data in a longitudinal study? How do you merge datasets from different collection instruments? How do you produce summary statistics that match APA or AMA table conventions?

The Research Lifecycle Structure

The book is structured around the phases of empirical research, not the features of pandas:

Part I — Foundations
Python, Jupyter, and pandas from zero. Data types, DataFrames, basic operations. Reading data from every format researchers encounter: CSV, Excel, SPSS .sav, Stata .dta, JSON, and database exports.

Part II — Cleaning and Preparation
The phase that consumes 60-80% of real research time. Missing data strategies, outlier detection and handling, data type coercion, string cleaning, date parsing, and the merge/join operations that combine data from multiple sources.

Part III — Analysis and Aggregation
Descriptive statistics, groupby operations, cross-tabulations, pivot tables, time-series analysis, and the pandas operations that correspond to the analyses researchers actually report.

Part IV — Reproducibility and Publication
Structuring notebooks for reproducibility. Generating publication-ready tables and figures. Version control basics. Sharing and archiving research data and code. Integration with reference managers and manuscript workflows.
