Audience: CEOs, project managers, engineering leaders, and anyone tired of dashboards that lie.

Dashboards showed green, stakeholders were reassured — and then, around month nine, reality arrived uninvited. What went wrong is rarely a question of effort. It is almost always a question of measurement.

This book is the product of thirty years of watching organisations confuse the performance of confidence for the substance of it. Drawing on empirical data from more than 200 real software projects, it introduces the Probabilistic Delivery framework: a systematic replacement for the story-point velocity theatre that dominates contemporary software governance.

What This Book Argues

The central thesis is simple and uncomfortable: software project failure is not a talent problem. It is a measurement problem. Organisations consistently use metrics that feel productive — velocity, burndown charts, sprint completion rates — while those metrics tell them nothing reliable about when software will actually be ready, at what quality, and at what cost.

The Probabilistic Delivery framework replaces these with actuarial tools borrowed from insurance and risk management: Monte Carlo simulation, reference class forecasting, P85 confidence thresholds, and the Binary Gate Protocol. These are not academic abstractions. They are tools that produce honest, defensible, probabilistic answers to the questions that actually matter.

Who This Book Is For

This book is written for three audiences who rarely read the same book:

Engineering leaders and CTOs who have inherited velocity-based planning and know, somewhere, that it isn’t working — but have no credible replacement to offer their organisations.

Project managers and programme directors who are caught between the optimism of development teams and the expectations of executives, and who need a framework that lets them report honestly without career risk.

CEOs and board members who are making multi-million dollar decisions based on dashboard data they have no way to interrogate. This book gives them the questions to ask and the framework to evaluate the answers.

The Probabilistic Delivery Framework

The framework rests on four pillars:

Reference Class Forecasting — rather than estimating from scratch, anchor forecasts in what similar projects have historically delivered. This is how actuaries price risk. It is how software governance should work.

Monte Carlo Simulation — replace single-point estimates (“we’ll be done in Q3”) with probability distributions (“there is a 15% chance we finish in Q3, a 62% chance in Q4, and a 23% chance it slips to Q1”). Decision-makers can act on distributions. They cannot responsibly act on single points they know are fiction.

The P85 Standard — commit to dates at which there is an 85% probability of delivery, not the 50% median that teams report when they say “Q3.” The P85 is the actuarial equivalent of a safety margin. It is the difference between governance and guesswork.

The Binary Gate Protocol — a structured decision framework that replaces the subjective “are we ready to ship?” conversation with measurable, pre-agreed criteria. Binary. Auditable. Honest.
